# Performance Review Process

**Owner:** People Team
**Last updated:** 2025-11-20
**Applies to:** All full-time employees

## Cadence

Northwind runs formal performance reviews twice a year, in June and
December. Each cycle has three components: a self-assessment, manager
assessment, and (for levels L4 and above) lightweight peer feedback from
2-3 colleagues you've worked closely with that cycle.

## Timeline for a typical cycle

- **Week 1**: Self-assessment opens. You write a summary of your key
  contributions, referencing specific projects and outcomes.
- **Week 2**: Peer feedback requests go out (L4+ only). Peers have one
  week to respond.
- **Week 3**: Manager writes their assessment, informed by your
  self-assessment and any peer feedback.
- **Week 4**: Calibration — managers within a department meet to discuss
  ratings together, to reduce inconsistency between different managers'
  standards. This is a normal and expected part of the process, not a
  sign that your manager doesn't trust their own judgment.
- **Week 5**: Review conversations happen 1:1 between you and your
  manager. Ratings and any compensation or promotion implications are
  discussed here.

## Ratings

We use a four-point scale: Exceeds, Strong, Meets, Below. The large
majority of employees land in "Strong" or "Meets" in a typical cycle —
these are not meant to be rare or exceptional ratings, despite how the
labels might sound. "Below" triggers a structured improvement
conversation with your manager and People Team involvement; it does not
automatically mean a performance improvement plan, though it can lead to
one if the pattern continues.

## Relationship to compensation and promotion

Performance ratings are one input into compensation decisions, but
compensation changes are not strictly mechanical based on rating — see
Compensation Bands Overview for how bands work. Promotion is a related
but distinct process with its own timeline and criteria; see Promotion
Process. It is possible (and reasonably common) to have a "Strong" rating
in a cycle without being promoted, since promotion typically requires
demonstrating sustained performance at the next level over multiple
cycles, not just one strong cycle.
